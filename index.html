DRIVING SIMULATOR

RWTH ISAC

2022-2025

This is the beginning of my professional coding experience and I've had to tackle some serious problems to meet the requirements of the researchers, who conceptualized the studies for the simulator.

My first task was to improve the dynamics of traffic vehicles, making their behaviour more physically accurate. I also optimized the vehicle rendering logic, significantly boosting performance by prioritizing the display of the most relevant cars among hundreds of simulated ones. Later, I integrated a Bluetooth heart-rate sensor that logs data to a database alongside numerous behavioural metrics from study participants. I was also responsible for implementing scenario logic for the two most recent simulator studies — the first of which was published in October 2025.
(Link to study in ScienceDirect.com)

Some of my smaller contributions included modelling local department vehicles, designing a new animated tachometer graphic for the simulator, and rendering short video clips for online forms.

Most recently, I’ve been developing an Arduino-based prototype for a new e-scooter simulator, transmitting physical sensor data via serial connection to Unity.

Finally, I have prepared a short trailer for the simulator showcasing the physical setup and a selection of entertaining clips I have made over the years.

OFFICE GAME PROTOTYPE

2025-2026?

This is the latest game I've started prototyping and is the first one I actually intend on finishing. While seemingly mundane at first there are a lot of fun features and many lines of code working behind the scenes to make everything run smoothly.

Initially I knew I wanted to have a desk, covered with stacks of paper, but after around a hundred sheets, the performance started to drop significantly. To fix that, I created container models that convey the feeling of giant paper stack without the performance hit. At the moment there are seven container sizes and the game dynamically chooses which ones to use to minimize the object count, similar to a shop clerk choosing the right coins to give as change.

The game can also generate a randomized grid of any size. Depending on the difficulty a certain percentage of the grid can include requirement zones for the player to fill in. These zones check for edge collisions when generating to prevent overlap in the final layout.

No office is complete without a bit of procrastination, so to replicate that experience, I added a procedural social media generator. It is loosely modeled after platforms such as Instagram or TikTok and allows for infinite scrolling, requiring custom code to get the smooth anchored-scrolling feel, which is iconic for the platforms. The displayed content is randomized, pulling from different sentence templates and word lists to create semi-coherent memes (which are arguably better than some 2025 memes).

Finally there are some meaningless interactables to round out the experience. When I looked at the nested structure of my scene, it reminded me of the six pivots of a Rubik's Cube, so I wanted to see if I could recreate this legendary toy using said parent-child relationships. There’s nothing too complex code-wise, but it’s something fun to click.
